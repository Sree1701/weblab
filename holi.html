Holi

Holi, or the "Festival of Colors," is an ancient Indian and Nepalese Hindu festival celebrating the arrival of spring, the triumph of good over evil, and the beginning of a new season of love and joy. Celebrated on the full moon day of the Hindu month of Phalgun (late February/early March), Holi involves a night of bonfires called Holika Dahan and a day of vibrant celebrations where people throw colored powders and water, sing, dance, and share festive sweets. The festival symbolizes forgiveness and the mending of relationships.

Holi is an ancient Hindu festival in India, celebrated as the "Festival of Colors," that marks the arrival of spring, the victory of good over evil, and the end of winter. It involves lighting bonfires on the first night to represent the destruction of evil, followed by a day of vibrant celebration where people, regardless of age or social status, playfully throw colored powder and water at each other. The festival also commemorates the divine love of Radha and Krishna and serves as a time for forgiveness and mending relationships

Holi is a yesteryear Hindu tradition and is also one of the most popular festivals in Hinduism. It was originated and predominantly celebrated in the Indian subcontinent and later spread to the Western world through the Indian diaspora. The festival of colour has various religious and spiritual significance. It celebrates the eternal and divine love of Lord Krishna and Radha. And on the other hand, the day also signifies the triumph of good over evil, as it commemorates the victory of Lord Vishnu as Narasimha Narayana over Hiranyakashipu.
